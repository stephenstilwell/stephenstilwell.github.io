Chapter V

A Letter to the Engineers and Builders of This Age

1From Stephen, a servant of honest work, to the engineers, the builders, the architects of systems, the solvers of hard problems — grace and clarity be unto you. 2I write to you as one of your own. I have sat in your meetings and read your specifications and argued over your requirements and shipped your products and felt the particular satisfaction of a thing that worketh as it was designed to work. 3There is no satisfaction quite like it. Hold onto it. The world will try to replace it with metrics. 4You are the ones who translate the dream into the thing. Without you, the vision of the entrepreneur is only a pitch deck. Without you, the promise of the salesman is only noise. 5And yet you are often the last to be consulted and the first to be blamed. This is an old injustice and it will not be corrected in this letter, but let it be named plainly so that you know you are seen. 6Stephen saith unto the engineer: do not shrink thyself to fit the room. Thy precision is not stubbornness. Thy insistence on doing it correctly is not obstruction. These are virtues dressed in clothing that maketh the impatient uncomfortable. 7There will be managers who ask thee to cut corners in the name of speed. Sometimes they will be right. Often they will not be. Learn to know the difference, and when thou knowest it, speak. 8A bridge built in haste is not a bridge. It is a promise made to gravity. 9The same is true of software. Of aircraft. Of ships. Of all the systems upon which human lives depend. Speed is a virtue only until the moment it becometh a gamble with another man's safety. 10Stephen hath worked in aerospace and defense and maritime and rail, and in all of it he hath observed that the disasters which make the news were almost always preceded by warnings that were not heeded. 11Someone knew. Someone always knew. And the knowing was uncomfortable, and the speaking of it was more uncomfortable still, and so the knowing was buried beneath the urgency of schedules and the pressure of budgets. 12Be the one who speaketh. Even when thy voice shaketh. Even when the room goeth quiet. Even when thou art the youngest person at the table. 13For the table would rather be uncomfortable for an hour than wrong for a lifetime. 14Now a word on the new age of machines that think, or that appear to think. Stephen hath watched the arrival of artificial intelligence with the eyes of an engineer, and he saith this: 15It is a powerful tool. It is not a replacement for thee. A tool that can draft a document cannot take responsibility for what the document builds. Thou canst. 16Do not worship the new tool. Do not fear it either. Understand it, as thou wouldst understand any system — its inputs and its outputs, its tolerances and its failure modes. 17The engineer who understandeth the machine is its master. The engineer who feareth the machine is its servant. The engineer who believeth the machine is its fool. 18Stephen saith also this: the loneliness of the builder is real and it is rarely spoken of. 19To see a problem clearly that others cannot yet see. To hold the full complexity of a system in thy mind whilst others see only its surface. To care deeply about the quality of a thing in a world that careweth mainly about its price. 20This loneliness is the price of the gift. Pay it without resentment. 21Find thy people — those who light up when the problem is hard, who stay late not because they are told to but because the thing is not yet right and they cannot leave it unright. 22These are thy tribe. They are rarer than gold and worth more. 23Finally, remember why thou beganst. Not for the title. Not for the salary, though the salary is just and thou shouldst pursue it without apology. 24Thou beganst because something in thee looked at the world and saw that it could be better ordered, better built, better understood. That instinct is sacred. Do not let the industry grind it out of thee. 25Build worthy things. Build honest things. Build things that work when no one is watching and hold together when the pressure cometh. 26That is the covenant of the engineer. It hath no ceremony and no ordination. But it is as holy as any I have known. 27Go well. Build well.
