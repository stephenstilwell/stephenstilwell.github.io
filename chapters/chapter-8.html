Chapter VIII

Proverbs on Money, Markets, and the Love of Gold

1Money is a good servant and a catastrophic god. Know always which it is in thy house on any given day. 2The man who knoweth how much he needeth is freer than the man who hath more than he can count, for the first man hath a destination and the second hath only a direction. 3Debt is borrowed time dressed in borrowed currency. Repay it with the same urgency thou wouldst repay a debt of honor, for it is one. 4A budget is not a punishment. It is a map. And a man without a map is not free — he is merely lost with optimism. 5Stephen saith: I have been broke and I have been comfortable, and comfort is better. Let no man romanticize poverty who hath known it truly. 6And yet I have seen rich men sleep poorly and laborers sleep deeply, and I conclude that peace of mind is a currency that the market doth not trade in. 7The market is not wise. The market is a mirror of human fear and human greed, and it moveth accordingly. Treat it as such and thou wilt not be surprised by it. 8When all men are buying, ask why. When all men are selling, ask why. The answer to both questions is usually the same: because other men are doing it. 9This is not a strategy. This is a stampede. And stampedes care nothing for the direction they are headed. 10Bitcoin and gold and land and labor — all have value insofar as men agree they do. The agreement is the thing. Never forget that the agreement can change. 11A man who investeth what he cannot afford to lose hath not invested. He hath gambled and called it by a quieter name. 12Diversify not because thou art fearful but because thou art humble. No man knoweth the future of any single thing, and the man who acteth as though he doth will eventually prove himself wrong at great expense. 13The love of money is not evil because money is evil. Money is a tool and tools are neutral. The love of it is evil because it replaceth the love of things that cannot be replaced. 14Thou canst earn more money. Thou canst not earn more time. Spend accordingly. 15A man who holdeth his savings in fear will lose them slowly to inflation. A man who holdeth them in greed will lose them quickly to foolishness. The narrow road between them is called patience. 16Pay the craftsman what he asketh if his work is good, and pay him promptly. The man who haggleth the tailor and delays the plumber hath not saved money. He hath borrowed goodwill at a high interest rate. 17Stephen hath watched the markets of his age rise to great heights on the faith of men and fall to great depths on the fear of them, and he concludeth that the market is less a measure of value than a measure of mood. 18Give generously before thou art comfortable, for if thou waiteth until thou art comfortable thou wilt wait forever. There is always one more thing to secure before the generosity can begin. 19The tithe is not a tax. It is a reminder that what thou hast holdest was never entirely thine to begin with. 20Finally this: when thou art old and thy accounting is done, no ledger will record how much thou accumulated. But the people whose lives were made better or worse by thy choices will remember. And they will be thy truest balance sheet.
