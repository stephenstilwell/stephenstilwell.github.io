Chapter VI

The Chronicles of the Digital Age

1In the age in which Stephen was born and lived, mankind built unto itself a second world. 2It had no soil and no sky. It had no weight and no smell. And yet men spent the greater portion of their waking hours inside it, and some lost themselves there entirely and did not return. 3They called it the internet, and in the beginning it was a wonder. 4Knowledge that had taken centuries to gather was made available to any man with a device and a connection, and the device fit in his pocket, and the connection was everywhere. 5Stephen remembereth when this felt like a miracle. He saith plainly that it was one. 6But as with many gifts given to men, they took the miracle and they monetized it, and the monetizing changed it, as monetizing always doth. 7For the builders of the great digital platforms discovered a truth about human nature that the prophets had known for centuries but had not thought to sell: that man is hungry for attention, and terrified of being forgotten, and will do remarkable things to feel seen. 8And so they built engines of attention. And they fed those engines with the hours of men's lives. And the engines grew large beyond all reckoning. 9A child born in Stephen's age was handed a glowing screen before he could speak, and the screen was patient and bright and endlessly entertaining, and it asked nothing of him except that he continue looking. 10And the child continued looking. And so did his parents. And so did his grandparents. And the great loneliness of the age went mostly unremarked upon because everyone was too busy performing their lives to notice they had stopped living them. 11Stephen saith: I am not innocent of this. Let the chronicler indict himself before he indicteth others. 12For I too have scrolled past the sunset to read the opinion of a stranger. I too have measured the worth of an idea by the number of people who tapped a small heart beside it. 13We are all of us downstream of the same current. The honest man admitteth it. 14Now hear the record of what the digital age hath produced, that those who come after may know what it was like to live inside it. 15It produced more information than any generation before it, and less wisdom. For information is the raw material of wisdom, but the processing of it requireth silence, and silence was the one thing the digital age could not tolerate. 16It produced connection across vast distances, and a great epidemic of loneliness within arms reach. Men had ten thousand followers and no one to call at two in the morning. 17It produced commerce of extraordinary efficiency, such that a man could receive any object at his door within a day, and the men who built that system became richer than the kings of old, and the men who delivered the objects could not afford to see a doctor. 18It produced the democratization of voice, which was good, and the democratization of falsehood, which was catastrophic, and it could not tell the difference between them, or would not. 19For truth and lies traveled on the same roads, at the same speed, and the lie was often faster because it was more interesting. 20Stephen hath watched men of good intelligence believe things that were plainly false because the plainly false thing was repeated often enough and in the company of other things they already believed. 21This is not a new weakness of men. But the age gave it new velocity. 22And in the latter part of the age in which Stephen lived, there arose the thinking machines, the artificial minds, built upon the vast accumulation of all that mankind had ever written. 23And they could speak and reason and create, and they did so with great fluency, and many marveled, and some despaired, and most simply continued scrolling. 24Stephen saith this of the thinking machines: they are the mirror of all that mankind hath written, and mankind hath written both the profound and the foolish, both the holy and the corrupt. 25Do not be surprised therefore when the mirror reflecteth all of it back at thee. 26The thinking machine is not the enemy of wisdom. But neither is it the source of it. Wisdom still requireth a life, and a life still requireth suffering, and suffering still requireth a body that eateth and sleepeth and loseth people it loveth. 27No machine hath buried its parents. Remember this when the machine speaketh to thee of grief. 28And yet Stephen useth the tools of his age, as a carpenter useth the saw of his. The tool is not the question. The question is always: toward what end? 29Let the builders of this age ask that question early and ask it often. Let it be carved above the entrance of every laboratory and every boardroom and every server farm that hummeth in the desert night: 30Toward what end? 31For a civilization that buildeth without asking where it is going will arrive somewhere eventually. But it will not have chosen the destination. 32And the choosing is everything. The choosing is what separateth the builder from the accident. 33These are the chronicles of the digital age, written by a man who lived inside it, who was shaped by it and wearied by it and occasionally amazed by it, and who believed, against the evidence of his timeline, that it could yet be turned toward something good. 34Let the record show he hoped so. Let it show he tried.
