Chapter XI

On Returning — Of Faith Found Again in the Ruins

1There is a particular kind of returning that is nothing like the leaving. The leaving is sharp and decisive, a door closing. The returning is slow, and uncertain, and it happeneth in pieces, like light coming back into a room that was not suddenly darkened but grew dim so gradually that the man inside it barely noticed until he could no longer see his own hands. 2This is how Stephen returned. Not in a moment. Not on a road to Damascus with a great light and a voice and a falling down. Quietly. Over years. In the accumulation of small things that refused to be explained away. 3The order of the universe, which his physics training had shown him was unreasonable in its precision. The persistence of beauty in a world that had no obligation to be beautiful. The fact of love, which is the most stubborn argument for meaning that hath ever existed. 4And the child coming. Always the child coming, moving in the dark like a rumor of grace. 5Stephen saith: I did not return to faith because the questions were answered. They were not. The great questions remain open and he suspecteth they will remain so for the duration of his life and perhaps beyond it. 6He returned because he found that living without any answer was colder than living with an incomplete one. And because the incomplete answer, when he held it honestly, had a warmth to it that the cold never did. 7The faith he returned to is not the faith of his childhood. That faith was a garment fitted to a boy, and the man that grief and labor and time had made could not wear it anymore without tearing it at the seams. 8The faith he returned to is larger and quieter and less certain of its own details and more certain of its center. The center is this: that there is something rather than nothing, and that the something is not indifferent, and that love is not an accident of chemistry but a signal from the deepest nature of what is real. 9He cannot prove this. He offereth it not as proof but as testimony. The testimony of a man who looked for a long time and found that the absence he had mistaken for emptiness was in fact a waiting. 10To those who have never left the faith — Stephen honoreth thee. The staying is its own kind of faithfulness and he doth not diminish it. 11But he writeth this chapter for those who have wandered. Who sit outside the door of the thing they once believed and feel they have been gone too long to re-enter without explanation. 12There is no explanation required. The door openeth from the inside, and it hath been open the whole time, and the only thing that kept thee outside was the belief that thou hadst to earn thy way back in. 13Thou dost not. This is the central scandal of grace and it hath offended reasonable men for two thousand years. Stephen findeth it the most believable thing about the whole enterprise, because no one would have invented it. It is too impractical to be invented. It must have been revealed. 14The ruins in which Stephen found his faith again were not a church, though he hath returned to one. They were the ruins of his own certainties. The places where he had been sure and was proven wrong. Where he had been strong and found himself broken. 15It is always in the ruins. This is the consistent testimony of every honest person who hath written on the subject across every century. The faith that is found in comfort is fragile. The faith that is found in ruins is the kind that holdeth. 16Stephen is not finished being rebuilt. He saith this without embarrassment. The work is ongoing and he expecteth it will be ongoing until the last day, and he hath made his peace with the ongoing. 17For a man who waiteth to be fully repaired before he offereth himself to the world will wait his whole life and die unoffered. 18Go as thou art. Offer what thou hast. Trust that the gap between what thou art and what thou oughtest to be is not thine alone to close. 19This is the returning. Not an arrival at a destination but a turning of the face in the right direction. And the turning is enough. It hath always been enough. 20The ruins are not the end of the story. They are where the story finally becometh true.
