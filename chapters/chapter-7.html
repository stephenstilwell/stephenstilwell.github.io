The Book of Stephen

Chapter VII

A Letter to the Powerful and the Greedy

1From Stephen, a man of no great wealth and no great title, to those who have accumulated both beyond all reasonable need — hear this, and do not look away. 2I do not write to thee in envy. Envy is a small and exhausting emotion and Stephen hath not the patience for it. 3I write to thee because someone must, and the men around thee will not, because their comfort dependeth upon thy continued pleasure, and the truth is therefore the first casualty of thy company. 4So hear it plainly from one who owneth nothing thou couldst take from him. 5Thou hast been given much. Not merely by thine own effort, though thine effort may have been real. Thou hast been given the age thou wert born into, and the country that protected thee, and the infrastructure that carryeth thy goods, and the schools that educated thy workers, and the courts that enforceth thy contracts. 6These things were built by the labor and the taxes of ordinary men and women, most of whose names thou dost not know and wouldst not recognize. 7Thou art not therefore self-made. Thou art made by the world and thou owest the world accordingly. This is not socialism. This is arithmetic. 8Stephen hath watched the powerful of his age stand before the assemblies of the nation and speak of their genius while their workers slept in their cars in the parking lots outside their warehouses. 9He hath watched them accept the honors of philanthropy with one hand whilst lobbying against the wages of the poor with the other. 10He hath watched them weep for the suffering of the world in public and produce the instruments of that suffering in private. 11And the crowd applauded, because the crowd hath been taught that wealth is evidence of virtue, and this is one of the oldest and most destructive lies in the history of organized civilization. 12Stephen saith: wealth is evidence of wealth. Nothing more. A man may be rich because he was wise, or because he was ruthless, or because he was lucky, or because he inherited it from a man who was one of these things before him. 13The coin looketh the same in every case. Do not mistake the coin for the man. 14Now hear the particular indictment of this age. 15In the time of Stephen there were men whose personal treasure exceeded the annual production of entire nations, and in those same nations children went unfed, and the sick went unhealed, and the old went uncared for. 16And those men looked upon this and said: the system is working. 17Stephen saith: the system is working for thee. That is a different statement entirely. 18To the man who hath built his fortune upon the low wages of many — thou hast not created value. Thou hast transferred it. From the many to the one. And thou hast done it with the patience and precision of a man who knoweth exactly what he is doing. 19To the man who hath purchased the instruments of government and bent them to his profit — thou art not a patriot. Thou art a parasite dressed in a flag, and the difference is visible to everyone except the flag. 20To the man who hath poisoned the water or the air or the soil in the pursuit of margin — thou hast made a withdrawal from an account that belonged to all men, including those not yet born, and thou hast not asked permission, and thou hast not left a receipt. 21There will be a reckoning for this. Stephen doth not know its form. But he hath studied enough physics to know that systems do not absorb infinite pressure without response. 22A word now to the powerful who are not greedy, for they exist and they deserve acknowledgment. 23There are those who have built great things and shared the building. Who have looked upon their workers as partners and not as costs. Who have used their platform to speak inconvenient truths rather than convenient ones. 24They are rarer than they should be, and the world is measurably better for each of them. Stephen nameth no names, for naming inviteth the corruption of flattery. But thou knowest who thou art. 25Continue. The pressure against thee is real and the temptation to join the other kind is constant and the rewards for doing so are immediate and large. 26Resist. The long table is longer than the quarterly report. 27And now the final word, which is not a condemnation but an invitation. 28Stephen believeth that most men do not begin greedy. They begin afraid. Afraid of scarcity, afraid of powerlessness, afraid of returning to whatever smallness they climbed out of. 29And the accumulation of wealth feeleth like the accumulation of safety, until the day it doth not, and on that day a man findeth himself with everything he pursued and nothing he needed. 30If thou art that man — and some who read this will be — then know that the returning is available to thee also. 31Not the returning of thy money, though some of that would do the world no harm. 32The returning of thy attention. To the things that do not appear on a balance sheet. To the faces of the people around thee. To the question of what thou art actually for. 33The powerful man who asketh that question honestly and answereth it honestly is more dangerous to the darkness of this age than any law that hath been written. 34Ask it. Answer it. Then act accordingly. 35That is all Stephen hath to say to the powerful. He hopeth they read it. He suspecteth their assistants will filter it out before it reacheth them. 36But God seeth past the assistant. Let it be written that Stephen tried.
