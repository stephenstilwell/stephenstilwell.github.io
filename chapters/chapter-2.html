Chapter II

The Proverbs of Stephen on Work and Men

1A man who knoweth his craft and speaketh plainly of it is worth ten men who speaketh grandly of nothing. 2Labor is not a curse, though many have made it one. The curse is not the work itself, but the master who taketh the fruit of it and calleth it his own. 3If thou wouldst know a man's character, observe not how he treateth those above him, but how he treateth those who can do nothing for him. 4The engineer who humbleth himself before the problem will solve it. The engineer who humbleth himself before his supervisor will only appear to. 5There is a kind of busyness that produceth nothing, and a kind of stillness that buildeth empires. Learn the difference early and thou shalt not waste thy years. 6Stephen labored among the builders of aircraft and the keepers of ships and the layers of rail, and in all of it he observed one truth: the men closest to the work knew the most, and the men furthest from it made the most decisions. 7This is a great foolishness, and it persisteth in every age. 8Beware the man who hath never built a thing telling thee how a thing should be built. 9A contract signed in haste is a snare dressed in the clothing of opportunity. 10The merchant who honoreth his word above his margin shall have few quick profits and many lasting ones. 11And the merchant who honoreth his margin above his word shall have many quick profits and an empty table in the end. 12Do not despise the salesman, for he who cannot communicate the value of a thing leaveth good work unseen in the darkness. But do not fully trust him either. 13Stephen saith: I have closed deals and I have lost them, and I have learned more from the losing. 14For in losing thou art shown the thing thou didst assume, and assumption is the enemy of excellence. 15There is a man who will smile at thee in the meeting and speak against thee in the hallway. Mark him early. He will not change. 16And there is a man who will speak hard truth to thy face and defend thee behind thy back. Keep him close all the days of thy life. 17A title is a garment. Some men fill it. Most men hide inside it. 18If thy employer asketh thee to do the work of three men for the wages of one, and calleth it opportunity, thou art being deceived. An opportunity profiteth both parties. 19Stephen hath known the bitterness of unpaid wages and the coldness of those who smile whilst they steal. Let it be written that such men exist, that the young worker may not be surprised when he meeteth them. 20Yet bitterness is a poor companion on a long road. Take the lesson. Pursue what is just. Then set thy face forward. 21The man who loveth his work but leaveth no time for those he loveth hath built a monument on an empty foundation. 22Rest is not laziness. The Lord himself rested, and he had rather more to do than any of us. 23A new industry will always need men who can speak both the language of the builder and the language of the buyer. Be that man and thou shalt never hunger long. 24Do not wait until thou art ready, for readiness is a horizon — it moveth as thou approachest it. Begin the work and become ready through the doing of it. 25Finally, remember this: no man on his deathbed hath ever wished he attended more meetings.
