Chapter IX

The Chronicles of the American Nation in His Time

1Let it be recorded that Stephen was born in the latter half of the twentieth century in the United States of America, a nation conceived in the language of liberty and delivered in the practice of contradiction. 2He loved it. He quarreled with it. He buried his dead in its soil and raised his children beneath its skies and paid his taxes into its treasury and voted in its elections, even when the choices made him despair. 3This is the chronicle of that nation as he knew it, and it is offered not in contempt but in the harder emotion: the grief of a man who expected more and kept expecting it anyway. 4In the time of Stephen the nation was divided, and the division was not merely of opinion but of the very story each half told about who it was and who its enemies were. 5And the stories had grown so far apart that the two halves no longer shared a common set of facts from which to argue, and without common facts there is no argument — only noise wearing the clothing of debate. 6Stephen hath watched his countrymen choose their facts the way they choose their teams, with loyalty preceding evidence, and he hath concluded that this is not a failure of intelligence but a failure of formation. 7A people not taught how to think will be taught what to think, and they will be grateful for the instruction, because certainty is comfortable and doubt is not. 8Now hear the particular tensions of the age in which Stephen lived. 9It was an age of great material abundance and spiritual poverty. Men had more than any generation before them had dreamed of having, and reported being miserable at historically high rates, and could not explain the gap between the two. 10It was an age in which the rights of the individual were spoken of as sacred, and in the same breath the common good was treated as a conspiracy, and no one found the contradiction remarkable. 11It was an age in which men confused the market with God — trusting it to solve problems it had created, worshipping its verdicts as justice, and calling its failures the fault of those who had suffered them. 12It was an age of great courage in the margins and great cowardice in the centers of power, and the margins went mostly unacknowledged and the cowardice went mostly unnamed. 13Stephen watched men of high office choose their offices over their honor, and he understood why, and he did not forgive it. 14For the temptation to preserve thy position by abandoning thy principles is as old as power itself, and history hath never once recorded this as wisdom. 15And yet there were those in his age who held. Who stood in the room and said the inconvenient thing. Who chose, when the choosing was costly, to choose well. 16Stephen nameth none of them here, for the naming of heroes is often the first step in their domestication. But he knoweth who they are, and they know who they are, and that is sufficient. 17A word now on the founding of the nation, which was debated in Stephen's time with more heat than light. 18The founders were men — specific, flawed, brilliant, compromised men. Some held enslaved people whilst writing that all men are created equal, and this is not a small thing and it cannot be made small by the greatness of the document. 19And yet the document was great. The ideals were real. The aspirations were worth the centuries of struggle required to approach them. 20Stephen saith: a nation built on an unmet promise is not a fraud. It is an obligation. The promise must be pursued, not abandoned, and not pretended already fulfilled. 21The honest patriot is the one who holdeth the nation to its own words. Not the one who insisteth the words were always fully honored. That man is not a patriot. He is a flatterer of the dead. 22Stephen believeth in the nation. He hath seen its people at their worst in the loud places and at their best in the quiet ones. The quiet ones outnumber the loud ones, and they always have. 23He hath seen neighbors carry each other through disasters that no policy could have anticipated. He hath seen strangers become community in the aftermath of things that broke the news cycle and then were forgotten by it. 24That is the nation Stephen loved. Not the one on the screen. The one on the street. The one in the hospital waiting room and the school gymnasium and the church basement and the union hall. That nation endureth. He hopeth forward. He worketh toward forward. That is the most any honest citizen can say.
