Chapter IV

On Doubt, and Why It Is Not Sin

1There is a teaching that hath done great damage in the house of faith, and it is this: that to doubt is to betray. 2Stephen saith unto thee plainly — it is not so. 3For doubt is not the opposite of faith. Indifference is the opposite of faith. Doubt is faith wrestling in the dark, and wrestling is not the same as fleeing. 4Consider Jacob, who wrestled with the angel of the Lord through the whole of the night, and would not release him, and was wounded in the wrestling, and was blessed because of it. 5The wound and the blessing came together. Remember this. 6A man who hath never doubted hath never truly believed. He hath only inherited an opinion and called it conviction. 7Stephen studied physics, and in physics there is a law: a thing untested is a thing unproven. The same holdeth for the soul. 8Put thy faith in the furnace of honest questioning. What surviveth the furnace is real. What burneth away was only assumption wearing the garments of belief. 9There are those who will tell thee that questions are dangerous. Stephen saith: only weak structures fear examination. The truth hath no reason to be afraid of thee. 10When Stephen lost his parents and turned from God, it was not because he stopped caring about the truth. It was because he cared about it too much to pretend. 11And there is more honor in that than in a lifetime of comfortable Sundays. 12God is not fragile. Thou canst not wound him with thy questions. He hath heard harder ones than thine, from men far holier than either of us. 13Job demanded an audience with the Almighty and received one. The Lord did not rebuke him for his boldness. He rebuked the friends who had offered tidy explanations in place of honest presence. 14Beware the man who hath an answer for every suffering. He hath mistaken theology for comfort and given thee neither. 15There is a kind of faith that requireth certainty, and it is a brittle thing. One hard frost and it cracketh. 16And there is a kind of faith that holdeth its questions in one hand and its trust in the other, and walketh forward anyway. This is the faith worth having. 17Stephen saith: I do not know why the righteous suffer. I do not know why prayer sometimes moveth mountains and sometimes moveth nothing at all. 18I do not know why the universe is precisely ordered enough to be described by mathematics, and yet wild enough to break the hearts of those who live inside it. 19These are not small questions. They are the largest questions there are. And anyone who telleth thee they have been fully answered is selling thee something. 20But here is what Stephen hath found, returning after the long wandering: the questions themselves are a form of prayer. 21To shake thy fist at the sky and demand an answer is still to address the sky. And the sky is not nothing. 22Doubt kept Stephen honest. It kept him from the comfortable lie, from the easy pew, from the faith that asketh nothing and therefore receiveth nothing. 23And when the returning came, it came not as certainty. It came as willingness. As a man who saith: I do not have all the answers, but I am willing again to seek them in this direction. 24That is enough. It hath always been enough. 25Let the doubter therefore not condemn himself. Let him instead follow his doubt all the way down to the bottom of it, and see what he findeth there. 26Stephen findeth, at the bottom of all his doubting, something that will not go away. A stillness. A sense that he is known. A quiet that is not emptiness. 27Call it what thou wilt. He chooseth to call it God, because nothing else he hath encountered hath fit the shape of it. 28And so he returneth. Wounded and blessed, like Jacob. With a limp and a new name. 29The name is: a man still trying. And it is enough.
