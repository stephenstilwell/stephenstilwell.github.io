The Book of Stephen

Chapter X

A Letter to His Child, Not Yet Born

1From Stephen, thy father, who hath not yet seen thy face but hath already reorganized everything in his heart to make room for thee — peace be unto thee, little one. Peace above all things. 2Thou art coming into a complicated world. I will not deceive thee about this. But thou art also coming into a world of extraordinary beauty, and I intend to show thee both, and to let thee decide what to do with the knowledge of each. 3Thou art of Italian blood and Florida sun, the same as I am. Thou hast in thee the stubbornness of people who crossed oceans with nothing and built something from the crossing. Remember this on the days when the crossing is hard. 4I have to tell thee something important before thou arrivest: thou art coming to a father who is still figuring himself out. I did not solve everything before thy arrival. I had hoped to. 5But I think now that a father who pretendeth to be finished is a less useful father than one who showeth thee honestly how a person continueth to grow. So that is what I will try to do. 6I lost thy grandparents before thou couldst know them, and this griefeth me on thy behalf as much as my own. They would have loved thee with the particular ferocity of grandparents, which is a love with all the urgency and none of the discipline. 7I will tell thee of them often. I will make sure thou knowest their names and their faces and the particular way they moved through the world. They will be real to thee. I promise thee this. 8A word on the world thou art entering: it is loud. It will attempt from thy earliest days to tell thee who thou art and what thou shouldst want and what thou shouldst fear. Most of what it telleth thee will be in service of someone else's interest. 9Learn early to sit in silence. To hear thy own thoughts before thou hearest the thoughts of the crowd. This is the most countercultural act available to a person in this age, and it will serve thee better than any skill I can teach thee. 10I cannot promise thee that life will be fair. I have studied enough of it to know it will not be, and I respect thee too much to begin our relationship with a comfortable lie. 11What I can promise thee is this: I will be there. Through the fair and the unfair both. On the days when the world maketh sense and on the days when it doth not. On the days when thou art proud of thyself and on the days when thou art not. 12I will not always know the right thing to say. But I will stay. And staying is the greater part of love. 13Thou art arriving at a moment when thy father is returning to his faith, and thou art part of the reason. For it is difficult to look upon the fact of thee — a whole new person, woven together in the quiet dark — and maintain that there is nothing behind the weaving. 14I do not know what thou wilt believe when thou art grown. That will be thy own wrestling, thy own long night, and I will not steal it from thee by deciding it too early on thy behalf. 15But I will show thee what wonder looketh like. And I will show thee what honesty looketh like. And I will show thee that doubt and love can occupy the same heart without destroying each other. 16The rest is thine to discover. 17Come safely. Come when thou art ready. The world is imperfect and thy father is imperfect and thy timing will be perfect regardless, because thou art already exactly what was needed. 18I will see thee soon. I am already looking forward to it more than I have words for, which is saying something, because as thou wilt come to learn — thy father always hath words.
