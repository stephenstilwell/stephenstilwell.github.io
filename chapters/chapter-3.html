The Book of Stephen

Chapter III

The Lamentation for His Mother and Father

1There are griefs that can be spoken, and there are griefs that sit below all language, in the place where the body remembereth what the mind cannot hold. 2This chapter is for them. For the mother of Stephen, and for his father. Who lived, and were loved, and were taken. 3Both were consumed by the sickness that eateth from within, that respecteth no goodness of a person, that heareth no argument and accepteth no bargain. 4And Stephen watched, as children watch, with helpless hands and a heart that did not know yet how much it would carry afterward. 5O my mother. Thou wert the first voice I knew and the first warmth, and when thou wert gone the world became a place I had to learn again from the beginning. 6I looked for thee in small things. In the smell of a kitchen. In the particular light of a Florida afternoon. In the way certain music moveth through a room and leaveth no one unchanged. 7Thou art in all of it still. This I believe. I did not always believe it, but I do now. 8O my father. Thou wert the measure by which I measured myself, and when thou wert gone I found I did not know what I was building toward. 9A son needeth his father longer than either of them knoweth. This is a truth no one telleth thee until the need is already past. 10I carried thy name and thy blood into rooms thou never saw, and I did work thou never knew about, and there were moments of victory when the first thing I wished was that I could tell thee. 11And thou wert not there. And that silence hath a particular weight that decreaseth not with years. 12Stephen saith: I was angry at God. Let it be written plainly. 13I said unto the silence: If thou art there, and if thou art good, then explain this to me. Explain the wasting of a body. Explain the theft of years. Explain why the prayers of a child availeth nothing. 14And the silence remained silent. And I took that silence for absence. 15I know now that grief speaketh too loudly for any other voice to be heard in its season. This is not God's failing. It is the nature of sorrow. 16But I did not know it then, and so I wandered. And the wandering was long. 17I do not regret the wandering entirely, for a faith that hath never been lost and found again is a faith that hath never been truly tested. 18Mine hath been tested in the fire, and what remaineth is not the faith of my confirmation, not the faith of a boy in a church who did not yet know what he was agreeing to. 19What remaineth is smaller and harder and more honest. Like a stone that hath been in a river a long time. 20To those who read this and have also buried a parent — or both — Stephen saith only this: thou art not alone in the questioning. Thou art not faithless for having grieved past the acceptable season. There is no acceptable season. 21Grieve as long as thou must. And when the day cometh that thou rememberest them and smilest before thou weepest, receive that day as a gift. 22It will come. I promise thee it will come. 23I set no flowers upon a grave and call it enough. I write these words instead, that their lives may be acknowledged in a place that outlasteth flowers. 24Rest, both of you. I am finding my way back. I think you would be glad of it.
